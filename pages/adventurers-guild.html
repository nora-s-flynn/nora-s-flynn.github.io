Welcome to the Player-Run Adventurer's Guild

This is a project to allow DMs a fun side system for high-level PCs with a lot of NPC friends. The central idea is to take variables readily available at the table and use an online calculator to generate a D100 table showing the chance of success for any given NPC adventuring team.

The Process

Find a Kidnapped Construct

Level 2 Intrigue Mission: 1 Fighter, 1 Rogue, 1 Wizard

Critical Success: A small pet construct +10RP

Success: 1800gp + A magic item from Table B + 8RP

Mixed Outcome: 1575gp + A magic item from Table A +2RP

Failure: -200gp expenses

Failure Requiring Intervention: The kidnapper has also kidnapped a party member!

When an agent joins your guild, sort them into a group based on the classic four D&D classes:

Fighter, Rogue, Wizard, Cleric

These can easily be adapted for use with themed guilds. A thieves' guild might use Brute, Thief, Mage, Spy instead, and focus mostly on the Crime missions.

We use levels 1-5, which equate to about 2 class levels each. A level 5 agent cannot progress further from working for your guild.

Next, decide who will go on this mission. You may select as many as the mission asks for, as well as an extra one. If an agent is paid per mission, they may be busy with other work, see payment section.

For example, let's say you send:

  • Durkon, Level 4 Cleric
    The most experienced of your guild, here to provide leadership to your underleveled team, paid per mission
  • Minrah, Level 1 Cleric
    The least experienced, in need of the training, on retainer
  • Vaarsuvius, Level 2 Wizard
    A solid pick and reliable in these sorts of encounters, on retainer
  • Haley, Level 2 Rogue
    Decent in a fight, paid per mission

The Average Party Level of this team is (4+1+2+2)/4 = 2.25, Durkon's experience makes up for Minrah.

The Team Suitability is given by matching the team to the example composition. Our example party has a Wizard and a Rogue, but no Fighter, so we have a Team Suitability of 2.

This mission takes place in Greysky City, and let's say the guild has a Reputation of -3 there. There are no extenuating factors affecting success, so the Situational Bonus is set to 0.

So we have the following input variables:

  • Mission Level = 2
  • Average Party Level = 2.25
  • Suggested Number of Agents = 3
  • Number of Agents Sent = 4
  • Team Suitability = 2
  • Guild Reputation = -3
  • Situational Bonus = 0

We plug these numbers into the calculator. It uses complicated functions to return the D100 table for the mission outcome, determined by the following input variables:

  • Mission Level - LM
  • Average Party Level - LP
  • Suggested Number of Agents - NM
  • Number of Agents Sent - NP
  • Team Suitability - S
  • Guild Reputation - R
  • Situational Bonus - B

There are also a number of controller variables built in to help a DM easily adjust how the calculator behaves. A customisation guide can be found here.

D100 Outcome
89-100 Critical success
39-88 Success
28-38 Mixed Outcome
10-27 Failure
0-9 Failure Requiring Intervention

The players roll when the agent team returns, which may be functionally immediately, or may depend on the distance the team needs to travel or the difficulty of the mission. You may choose to show the players how their choices have affected the odds.

  • A Critical Success usually means the guild agents overperform, and earn a special reward unique to the mission. This may include magic items, favours or agents.
  • A Success means the guild completes the task exactly as asked, and earns a decent reward usually including gold (gp) and Reputation Points (RP).
  • A Mixed Outcome implies that the point of the mission has been achieved, but with some downside. Generally similar to a Success but with a decreased reward.
  • A Failure means the object of the mission was not achieved, and generally involves a loss of RP and sometimes a loss of gold.
  • A Failure Requiring Intervention means that the guild didn't just fail, it also needs the guildmasters to intervene personally! If the PCs don't react to the situation in time, agents and NPCs may die.

Now we need to allocate experience!

An agent needs 12 XP to level up, and receives 1XP for each entry in the following list that they accomplish:

Experience
Is the mission's level within 2 of the agent's level? 1 XP
Is the mission's level within 1 of the agent's level? 1 XP
Is the mission's level higher than the agent's level? 1 XP
Did the mission have a mixed result or better? 1 XP
Did the mission have a successful result or better? 1 XP
Was the mission a critical success? 1 XP

If they've had a Mixed Outcome:

  • Durkon gets 2 XP - Not much that he hadn't seen before
  • Minrah gets 4 XP - On the way to level 2
  • Mialee gets 3 XP - Less of a learning experience for her
  • Nott gets 3 XP - As above, all in a day's work

If an agent is experienced and not on retainer, they may be taking other work. Roll their absence die to see if the agent is busy. On a 1, they are unavailable. You may not use them for this mission.

Reputation

This system uses a Reputation score for each Region in your setting, representing how NPCs view the guild.

As agents complete missions, the guild's reputation may change in the region the quest was completed.

This affects the odds of success of future missions in that Region, as well as how NPCs treat your PCs as they do their own adventuring:

Reputation
Reputation Score (RP) Attitude Means Possible Actions
40 ― 50 Helpful Will take risks to help you Protect, heal, aid, ask to join guild
10 ― 39 Friendly Wishes you well Chat, advise, advocate
-9 ― 9 Indifferent Doesn’t much care Socially expected interaction
-39 ― -10 Unfriendly Treats you with suspicion Gossip, avoid, watch, insult
-50 ― -40 Hostile Wishes you ill Attack, interfere, berate, flee

Every so often, average out reputation scores between neighbouring Regions as word spreads between them.

You can split your setting into as many or few Regions as you like, though be aware that the more regions there are, the more missions the guild will have to complete to make an impact across the setting as a whole.

Under the Functions tab in the calculator, you can find and change many things about how it behaves. If you have your own Desmos account, you can save your own version of the calculator that behaves just how you like it.

The calculator uses a variable m to determine where the boundaries that separate mission outcomes fall. This m is a complicated function of the input variables, which you're welcome to change.

Strength Factors

As it is now, there is an overall swing factor a that changes how easily the boundaries move. Increasing a will increase the overall swingyness of the system.

You can also change how much each individual effect changes the boundaries. There are the following strength factors:

  • a - Overall strength factor affecting swingyness
  • aL - Strength factor for being over/underlevelled
  • aP - Strength factor for having appropriate classes
  • aN - Strength factor for having the right number
  • aR - Strength factor for having a good reputation
  • aO - Strength factor for ad-hoc DM adjustments

By increasing the associated strength factor, you can increase how sensitively the system depends on that variable or effect.

Boundary Functions

There are 4 boundary functions that separate the different mission outcomes. Each evaluates to a number between 1 and 100:

  • fA - Separates 'Critical Success' from 'Success'
  • fB - Separates 'Success' from 'Mixed'
  • fC - Separates 'Mixed' from 'Failure'
  • fD - Separates 'Failure' from 'Failure Requiring Intervention'
Offsets

Each boundary is determined by a tanh function that depends on m, and each is shifted by an amount specific to that boundary function, called the offset. mA is the offset for fA and so on. Changing these offsets moves boundaries closer together or further aparet, determining how much harder it is to get one mission outcome than another.

Steepnesses

Steepnesses, for example kA, change how slow or fast each boundary changes with respect to m. The steepness fA is set to 0.5 while the rest are set to 1. This is so that, as the players throw more and more advantages at a mission, 'Critical Success' doesn't come to dominate the odds as quickly as 'Success' does.

  • Offsets (m), each outcome boundary is a separate tanh function, varying by offsetting each a given amount. Varying m moves boundaries closer together or further apart
  • Steepnesses (k), change how slow or fast the boundary changes with respect to m. Changing these can let one outcome grow faster or slower than the others.
Calibration

The default values of the Boundary Variables are set to give reasonable results in the following test case:

  • LP = LM = 5 - You're generally experienced enough
  • NP = NM = 5 - You're sending the right number of people
  • PP = 4 - Most of the roles asked for are covered
  • R = 0 - The region is neutral towards your party
  • O = 0 - There are no special circumstances to consider
